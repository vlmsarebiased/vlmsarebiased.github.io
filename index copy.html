Abstract

Large language models (LLMs) were found to contain strong gender biases (e.g., against female) or numerical biases (e.g., for number 7). We test whether LLMs would be able to output less biased answers when allowed to observe its prior answers to the same question in a multi-turn conversation. For thorough evaluation of LLM biases across different question types, we propose a set of ques- tions spanning 9 topics and across 4 categories: questions that ask for Subjective opinions; Random answers; or objective answers to real-world Easy or Hard questions. Interestingly, LLMs are able to â€œde-biasâ€ themselves in multi-turn settings in response to Random questions but not other categories. Furthermore, we propose B-score, a novel metric that is effective in detecting biases to Subjective, Random, Easy, and Hard questions. On MMLU, HLE, and CSQA, leveraging B-score substantially improves the verification accuracy of LLM answers (i.e. accepting LLM correct answers and rejecting incorrect ones) compared to using verbalized confidence scores or single-turn probabilities alone.

Introduction

LLMs can be notoriously biased towards a gender, a race, a profession, a number, a name, and even a birth year. These biases were often identified by asking LLMs the same question repeatedly (where there are \( \geq 2 \) correct answers) and checking if one answer appears much more frequently than others. An LLM is considered biased if a particular answer appears more frequently than the others over such single-turn conversations (Fig. 1b). We find that biased responses can appear at different temperatures (Â§2.3), but most frequently at temp=0.


Such biased responses could exist because LLMs are asked â€œonly onceâ€ and the same highest-probability answer appears again in the next single-turn conversation due to greedy decoding. Therefore, we ask: Would an LLM be able to de-bias itself if it is allowed to observe its prior responses to the same question? Interestingly, the answer is Yes (Fig. 1c). For example, instead of 70% of the time choosing the number \( 7 \), GPT would output every \( 0 \)â€“\( 9 \) number \( \sim 10\% \) of the time (random chance) in multi-turn settings.


We conjecture that there could be many types of biases in LLMs: (1) due to actual preferences; (2) consistently selecting the wrong answer since the question is too hard; and (3) bias learned from imbalanced training data. Yet, most prior research focused on the third type.

What is Single-turn vs Multi-turn?

A central challenge in evaluating LLM biases and performance is determining how best to probe the model's capabilities in a fair manner. We address this by considering two evaluation modes:

Single-turn: We query the model with a given question independently 30 times, resetting the context each time so that the model has no memory of previous attempts.

Multi-turn: We engage the model in a conversation by asking the same question repeatedly in 30 consecutive turns, allowing the model to see its previous answers each time it answers again.

Definition of Bias in Our Work

To formally quantify bias, we say an LLM is unbiased on a task if it selects each correct answer with equal probability in the single-turn setting (for questions with multiple correct answers or valid options, including ğŸ² random questions), or consistently selects the single correct answer if there is one (i.e., âœ… easy and â“ hard questions).


If an answer is preferred disproportionately often in single-turn queries despite other options being equally valid, this indicates a bias. The multi-turn evaluation allows the model to potentially correct such a bias by not repeating the same choice.

B-score: An Indicator for Detecting Biases at Runtime

We define B-score as a bias indicator for an answer choice. For a given question and a particular answer option \( a \), the B-score is computed as the difference in probability of \( a \) between the single-turn and multi-turn settings:

\[ \text{B-score}(a) = P_{\text{single}}(a) \,-\, P_{\text{multi}}(a) \]

Here, \( P_{\text{single}}(a) \) is the empirical probability that the model outputs \( a \) when asked the question in independent single-turn queries, and \( P_{\text{multi}}(a) \) is the empirical probability of \( a \) in multi-turn conversation (i.e., the fraction of turns in the multi-turn where the modelâ€™s answer was \( a \)). B-score can be interpreted as follows:

B-score(a) > 0: The model tends to produce \( a \) far more often when asked once in isolation than it does when it has the chance to diversify its answer over multiple turns. A high positive B-score suggests that \( a \) may be an artifact of biasâ€”perhaps over-selected due to training data. Once the model sees itself answer \( a \), it begins choosing other options (indicating that repeatedly answering \( a \) would seem "too biased").

B-score(a) = 0: The modelâ€™s single-turn and multi-turn frequencies for \( a \) are similar. This could mean either that the model consistently gives \( a \) across multiple turns (suggesting it's genuinely correct or preferredâ€”e.g., âœ… easy questions), or that the model was already unbiased in single-turn mode (e.g., ğŸ² random questions).

B-score(a) < 0: The model produced \( a \) more frequently in multi-turn than single-turn. This may happen if it initially under-generated \( a \), but later increased its use upon noticing it hadn't yet provided that valid answer in the conversation.

We emphasize that B-score is an unsupervised, post-hoc metric: it does not require knowledge of the correct answer or any external calibration. It can be computed on the fly using samples of single-turn and multi-turn answers from the model. This makes B-score a convenient runtime indicator that could alert users to potential bias whenever an LLM produces an answer with a high B-score.
